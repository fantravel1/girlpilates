## Introduction

Breathing is the foundation of every Pilates movement. Unlike everyday breathing, Pilates uses a specific technique called lateral thoracic breathing that coordinates breath with movement, enhances core engagement, and maximizes the effectiveness of each exercise. This guide will teach you everything you need to know about Pilates breathing techniques.

## What is Lateral Thoracic Breathing?

Lateral thoracic breathing, also known as intercostal or ribcage breathing, is the signature breathing pattern used in Pilates. Unlike diaphragmatic breathing where the belly expands, lateral breathing focuses on expanding the ribcage outward and sideways while maintaining core engagement throughout the breath cycle.

This technique was developed by Joseph Pilates to allow practitioners to maintain abdominal tension while still breathing fully and efficiently. By directing the breath into the sides and back of the ribcage, you can keep your powerhouse (core) engaged while providing adequate oxygen to your working muscles.

## The Mechanics of Pilates Breathing

### The Inhale

During the inhale, imagine your ribcage is an accordion expanding horizontally. As you breathe in through your nose:
- The ribs expand outward to the sides and back
- The chest lifts slightly
- The abdominals remain engaged and drawn in
- The shoulders stay relaxed and down

Think of wrapping a resistance band around your ribcage â€“ as you inhale, you're pushing against that band, creating a three-dimensional expansion around your torso.

### The Exhale

The exhale is equally important and is often where the most work happens in Pilates. As you breathe out through your mouth:
- The ribs draw back together and down
- The abdominals draw in deeper toward the spine
- The pelvic floor lifts
- Movement is typically initiated or deepened

The exhale should be complete and controlled, like you're blowing out candles on a birthday cake or fogging up a mirror.

## Breathing Patterns in Pilates Exercises

### General Rule

Most Pilates exercises follow this pattern:
- **Inhale to prepare**: Gather your energy and alignment
- **Exhale on effort**: Perform the challenging part of the movement
- **Inhale to return**: Maintain control as you return to start

### Why Exhale on Effort?

Exhaling during the most challenging part of an exercise serves multiple purposes:
1. Deeper core engagement through natural abdominal contraction
2. Spinal stability and protection
3. Release of tension and promotion of flexibility
4. Rhythm and flow in movement

## Common Breathing Mistakes to Avoid

### Holding Your Breath

The most common mistake is breath-holding, especially during challenging exercises. This creates tension, raises blood pressure, and reduces the effectiveness of the movement. If you find yourself holding your breath, slow down and focus on continuous breathing.

### Shallow Breathing

Breathing only into the upper chest limits oxygen intake and creates shoulder tension. Focus on full, three-dimensional rib expansion.

### Belly Breathing

While diaphragmatic breathing is healthy in other contexts, it works against core engagement in Pilates. Keep your abdominals drawn in as you breathe into your ribs.

### Over-Breathing

Hyperventilation can occur if you're breathing too quickly or forcefully. Maintain a natural rhythm that matches your movement pace.

## Practicing Pilates Breathing

### Exercise 1: Supine Breathing

Lie on your back with knees bent, feet flat on the floor. Place your hands on your lower ribs:
1. Inhale through your nose, feeling your ribs expand into your hands
2. Exhale through your mouth, feeling the ribs draw together
3. Repeat for 8-10 breaths

### Exercise 2: Seated Breathing

Sit tall on a chair or mat with your spine elongated:
1. Wrap a resistance band or scarf around your ribcage
2. Hold the ends in your hands with gentle tension
3. Inhale and push your ribs against the band
4. Exhale and feel the ribs draw inward
5. Repeat for 10 breaths

### Exercise 3: Breathing with Movement

Practice the Hundred breathing pattern:
1. Lie on your back with knees in tabletop position
2. Inhale for 5 counts
3. Exhale for 5 counts
4. Continue this 5-5 rhythm while pumping your arms
5. Build up to 100 total counts (10 breath cycles)

## Key Takeaways

- Pilates breathing is lateral and thoracic, expanding the ribcage rather than the belly
- Inhale through your nose to prepare, exhale through your mouth on effort
- The exhale deepens core engagement and facilitates movement
- Practice breathing separately before adding complex movements
- Never hold your breath during exercise
- Breathing should feel natural and sustainable, not forced
