## The Scholar of Pilates

Kathy Grant brought something unique to the Pilates Elders: academic rigor. While others taught primarily in private studios, Kathy spent 28 years teaching Pilates at New York University, bringing the method into higher education and training generations of teachers with her distinctive analytical approach.

She was also one of the few Black teachers in the early Pilates world - a distinction that carried both challenges and significance.

## A Dancer's Path

Born Kathleen Stanford in 1921 in Boston, Kathy began dancing as a child and eventually pursued it professionally. She danced on Broadway and in nightclubs, navigating the segregated entertainment world of mid-century America.

Like many dancers, injuries brought her to Joseph Pilates. The encounter would shape the rest of her life.

## Learning from Joe

Kathy began studying with Joseph Pilates in the 1950s. She was one of only two people - along with Lolita San Miguel - to be certified by Joseph himself to teach his method.

This direct certification was rare. While many of the Elders trained extensively with Joseph, he didn't formally certify teachers. That he certified Kathy suggests the particular regard in which he held her abilities.

What Kathy learned from Joseph:
- The complete apparatus repertoire
- His philosophical approach to mind-body unity
- Teaching methodology and observation skills
- The importance of individual adaptation

She also absorbed something of his personality: demanding, precise, and intolerant of sloppiness.

## The NYU Years

After Joseph's death in 1967, Kathy took a path none of the other Elders chose: she joined academia.

### Bringing Pilates to Higher Education

In 1972, Kathy began teaching Pilates at New York University's Tisch School of the Arts. For 28 years, she trained dancers in the method, bringing academic structure to what had been purely apprenticeship-based learning.

This was revolutionary:
- **Legitimacy**: University affiliation gave Pilates academic credibility
- **Accessibility**: Students encountered Pilates as part of their education
- **Documentation**: Academic context encouraged analysis and preservation
- **Teacher Training**: She shaped how future teachers understood the method

### The Curriculum

Kathy developed a comprehensive curriculum that included:
- All apparatus work
- Anatomy and kinesiology
- Teaching methodology
- Movement analysis
- Her own supplementary exercises

## Innovations: "Before the Hundred"

Kathy's most famous contribution was her "Before the Hundred" - a series of preparatory exercises developed to prepare students for the classical Hundred exercise that traditionally opens the mat sequence.

### The Problem She Solved

The Hundred is demanding: it requires core strength, breath control, and cervical stability. Many beginners couldn't do it safely or effectively.

Rather than skip it or struggle through, Kathy created a progression:
- Exercises that built prerequisite strength
- Movements that established proper patterns
- Gradual progressions toward the full exercise

### The Approach

"Before the Hundred" reflected Kathy's analytical mind:
- **Breaking down components**: Identifying what the Hundred required
- **Sequencing intelligently**: Building skills in logical order
- **Meeting students where they are**: Not forcing bodies into forms they weren't ready for

This pre-exercise approach influenced therapeutic Pilates and beginner programming widely.

## Teaching Style

Those who studied with Kathy describe a formidable presence:

### Intellectual Rigor

Kathy wanted to know why things worked. She analyzed exercises anatomically, questioned traditions, sought understanding beyond "because Joe said so."

### High Expectations

Like Joseph, Kathy demanded excellence. She didn't accept sloppy work or lazy thinking.

### Precise Language

Words mattered to Kathy. She developed specific cueing language and expected teachers to use it precisely.

### Observation Skills

Kathy was famous for her ability to see what was happening in a body. Years of watching dancers had trained her eye.

### Homework

Unlike teachers who worked only in the studio, Kathy assigned homework - exercises to practice, readings to complete, concepts to master.

## The Question of Race

Kathy Grant was one of very few Black people in the Pilates world of her era. This reality shaped her experience in ways she rarely discussed publicly.

### Navigating Predominantly White Spaces

The Pilates world - like dance, like fitness - was predominantly white. Kathy navigated this with professionalism, focusing on her work rather than advocacy.

### A Quiet Pioneer

Her presence opened doors, even if quietly. Seeing a Black woman as a master teacher, certified by Joseph Pilates himself, mattered.

### Legacy for Diversity

Contemporary conversations about diversity in Pilates often invoke Kathy as a predecessor - someone who proved excellence has no color, even when the field didn't recognize that.

## The Grant Style

Kathy developed a distinctive approach sometimes called "Grant Work":

### Analytical Framework

She categorized exercises and understood their relationships systematically.

### Props and Variations

She was creative with props and modifications, developing exercises that addressed specific needs.

### Emphasis on Breathing

Like all the Elders, Kathy emphasized breath, but with her own particular attention to patterns and timing.

### Mind-Body Language

She developed rich vocabulary for discussing the internal experience of movement.

## Students and Legacy

Kathy trained hundreds of teachers at NYU and in private settings. Notable students include:

- Cara Reeser (prominent contemporary teacher and educator)
- Many NYU dance faculty
- Teachers who now lead their own training programs

Her influence spreads through these students into the broader Pilates world.

## Later Years

Kathy continued teaching until shortly before her death in 2010 at age 89. She remained sharp, demanding, and devoted to the work.

In her final years, she was recognized increasingly as a living treasure - someone who had actually learned from Joseph and could transmit that direct knowledge.

## Philosophy

Kathy's approach reflected distinctive values:

### Education Over Entertainment

Pilates was serious study, not just exercise. She expected intellectual engagement.

### Preparation Before Performance

Her "Before the Hundred" philosophy extended everywhere: prepare properly before attempting advanced work.

### Individual Attention

Despite teaching at a university, Kathy maintained focus on individuals. Each body needed specific attention.

### Language Matters

Precise language created precise movement. Sloppy cueing produced sloppy results.

## Key Takeaways

- Kathy was one of two people personally certified by Joseph Pilates
- She was one of few Black teachers in early Pilates history
- She taught at NYU's Tisch School for 28 years
- She developed "Before the Hundred" - preparatory exercises widely used today
- Her analytical approach brought academic rigor to Pilates
- She trained generations of teachers who spread her methodology
- She emphasized intellectual understanding alongside physical practice
- She taught until shortly before her death in 2010 at age 89
